# Contoso Corp Social Media and External Communications Policy

## Purpose

Contoso Corp operates in a world where a single post, comment, or offhand remark to a reporter can shape how customers, investors, and regulators perceive the company. This policy sets expectations for how employees use social media on their own time and how the company manages official external communications, so that our public voice remains accurate, consistent, and legally sound.

This policy applies to every Contoso Corp employee, contractor, and intern, regardless of role, level, or location, and it applies whether you are posting from a personal account, a professional networking profile, a public forum, or a company-provisioned device. It covers text, images, video, livestreams, and any other content shared to a platform that is visible outside Contoso Corp, including platforms that did not exist when this policy was last revised.

The policy exists to protect three things at once: our employees, who deserve clear rules rather than guesswork about what is safe to share; our shareholders and the investing public, who are entitled to receive material information about Contoso Corp through proper regulated channels rather than through informal leaks; and our brand, which took years to build and can be damaged quickly by a careless or malicious statement.

Because Contoso Corp is publicly traded on the Nasdaq Global Select Market under the ticker CTSO, several sections of this policy intersect directly with securities law, particularly rules against selective disclosure of material nonpublic information and insider trading. Employees who are unclear on how those rules interact with everyday social media use should treat any doubt as a reason to stay silent and ask Legal or Investor Relations first.

This policy works alongside, and does not replace, the Contoso Corp Code of Conduct and Business Ethics Policy, the Contoso Corp Equal Employment Opportunity and Anti-Harassment Policy, and the Contoso Corp Information Security and Acceptable Use Policy. Where those policies impose a stricter standard on a topic this document also touches, such as confidentiality of trade secrets or conduct toward coworkers, the stricter standard controls.

Nothing in this policy is intended to restrict activity protected by Section 7 of the National Labor Relations Act, including discussions among employees about wages, hours, or working conditions, nor is it intended to prevent any employee from reporting suspected legal violations to a government agency or from participating in a government investigation. Contoso Corp will not retaliate against employees for engaging in such protected activity.

Questions about whether a specific planned post, interview, speaking engagement, or public statement is appropriate should be directed to Corporate Communications at corpcomms@contoso-corp.example before the content is published, not after. It is far easier to review a draft than to walk back a post that has already been screenshotted and shared.

This policy is reviewed annually by Corporate Communications, Legal, and Human Resources, and updated on an ad hoc basis whenever a new platform, regulatory development, or internal incident reveals a gap that needs to be addressed sooner. The version posted on the Corporate Communications intranet page is always the controlling version, and employees should not rely on printed copies or older emailed summaries that may not reflect the current text.

Managers are expected to reinforce this policy during onboarding for new hires and during team meetings following any major company announcement, product launch, or public event where employee posting volume tends to spike. A manager who notices a team member's post veering into territory this policy covers should raise it directly and promptly rather than waiting for Human Resources or Corporate Communications to notice independently.

## Personal Social Media Use

Contoso Corp respects the right of every employee to maintain a personal presence on social media and to speak as a private individual about topics that matter to them, including topics related to their work in general terms. This policy does not ask employees to stop being people online; it asks that a few specific lines not be crossed.

Employees who identify themselves as working at Contoso Corp in a bio, profile, or post, and who then comment on the company, its products, its industry, or its competitors, must include a visible disclaimer making clear the views expressed are their own and do not represent the official position of Contoso Corp. A standard disclaimer such as 'Views are my own and do not reflect the views of my employer' placed in a profile bio or included in the relevant post satisfies this requirement.

A disclaimer does not provide cover for content that would otherwise violate this policy or any other Contoso Corp policy. Adding 'opinions are my own' beneath a post that discloses confidential financial information, harasses a coworker, or makes defamatory claims about a competitor does not make that content acceptable; the underlying conduct is still a policy violation regardless of the disclaimer.

Employees must never disclose confidential or proprietary information on personal social media accounts. This includes unreleased product features, internal roadmaps, unannounced acquisitions or partnerships, unpublished financial results, customer lists, contract terms, security vulnerabilities, internal reorganization plans, and anything else that has not been made public through an official Contoso Corp channel. This obligation is identical to the confidentiality obligations described in the Contoso Corp Code of Conduct and Business Ethics Policy and does not expire when an employee leaves the company.

Because Contoso Corp's stock is publicly traded, employees must take particular care with anything that touches financial performance. Do not post about quarterly revenue, margins, headcount reductions, large customer wins or losses, pending litigation outcomes, or any other information that a reasonable investor would consider important to a decision to buy or sell CTSO stock, until that information has been released publicly through a press release, Form 8-K, or earnings call. Sharing such information prematurely, even in a private group chat that later leaks, can constitute unlawful selective disclosure or insider trading, and Legal treats these situations with the highest severity.

Employees who hold material nonpublic information because of their role, such as members of the finance, legal, or executive teams, or anyone working on an unannounced deal, remain subject to the Contoso Corp insider trading blackout calendar and pre-clearance procedures regardless of what they post or do not post on social media; silence on social media does not substitute for compliance with those separate trading restrictions.

Employees are free to disagree publicly with a Contoso Corp product decision, business strategy, or public statement, but disagreement must stay respectful, must not reveal confidential deliberations, and must not be presented as an official company position. An employee frustrated with a feature decision may say so as a personal opinion; they may not claim to speak for the product team or imply that leadership agrees with them privately.

All conduct standards that apply inside the office apply equally online. Harassment, discriminatory remarks, threats, bullying, or demeaning comments directed at coworkers, customers, applicants, or competitors are prohibited on personal social media exactly as they are prohibited in the workplace, and violations are handled under the Contoso Corp Equal Employment Opportunity and Anti-Harassment Policy regardless of whether the conduct occurred on a company system or a personal phone at midnight.

This extends to conduct in private messages, closed groups, and anonymous or pseudonymous accounts if the account can reasonably be traced back to a Contoso Corp employee acting in connection with their employment, such as commenting on a coworker's public post, discussing internal team dynamics, or reacting to work-related news in a way that identifies the poster's employer.

Employees should never use Contoso Corp trademarks, logos, product screenshots, or brand assets on personal accounts to imply an endorsement or official capacity they do not have. Personal use of company photography from an internal event is generally fine for a one-off post crediting the event, but employees should not build a personal brand, side business, or influencer presence that trades on Contoso Corp's name without prior written approval from Corporate Communications.

Do not accept or solicit gifts, payments, or free products from customers, vendors, or partners in exchange for favorable social media coverage of Contoso Corp products, and disclose any sponsored content involving Contoso Corp products consistent with the Contoso Corp Code of Conduct and Business Ethics Policy's conflict-of-interest provisions and applicable advertising disclosure law.

Employees who leave a comment, review, or rating on a public site about a Contoso Corp product, a competitor's product, or a former employer while identifying themselves as a Contoso Corp employee should apply the same disclaimer and confidentiality expectations described above. Posting an anonymous negative review of a competitor while omitting your employment at Contoso Corp is also prohibited, as it constitutes deceptive marketing practice that could expose the company to regulatory or reputational risk.

Use good judgment about permanence. Deleting a post does not undo the damage of a screenshot already shared, and platform archives, web caches, and third-party scraping tools mean that almost nothing posted publicly is truly retractable. When in doubt about a post, wait, reread it as if a journalist or a family member were the audience, and if uncertainty remains, do not post it.

Employees using their personal devices for work purposes, including checking Contoso Corp email or calendar on a personal phone, remain subject to the data handling and device security expectations in the Contoso Corp Information Security and Acceptable Use Policy, and should not assume that keeping an activity 'off the work laptop' removes it from the scope of company policy.

Job-hunting posts, resignation announcements, and celebratory 'new job' posts are permitted and encouraged as a normal part of professional life, but employees who are still employed at Contoso Corp at the time of posting should avoid disparaging Contoso Corp, current teams, or managers in the process, and should avoid disclosing internal team structures, headcount, or reorganization details as part of explaining a departure.

Employees participating in open-source projects, technical forums, or developer communities on their own time may discuss general technical topics and even reference having worked on similar problems at Contoso Corp, but must not share proprietary source code, internal architecture diagrams, internal tooling names, or performance benchmarks obtained through their employment unless that material has already been published externally by Contoso Corp itself.

Livestreaming, including gaming streams, vlogs, and other real-time broadcast formats, carries elevated risk because content cannot be reviewed before it airs. Employees who livestream regularly and identify as Contoso Corp employees should be especially cautious about screen-sharing anything connected to a work device, since a background notification, an open internal tab, or a stray comment can disclose confidential information before anyone has a chance to catch it.

Contoso Corp does not monitor employees' personal social media accounts as a matter of course and has no interest in policing employees' political views, hobbies, or personal lives. Enforcement of this policy is triggered by specific reports, complaints, or content that comes to the company's attention, not by proactive surveillance of personal accounts.

Employees who are unsure whether a post they are considering would cross a line described in this section are encouraged to ask Corporate Communications or their manager before posting rather than after, and asking the question is never treated as an admission of wrongdoing or held against the employee in any way.

## Speaking on Behalf of Contoso

Only specifically designated employees may communicate publicly as an official voice of Contoso Corp. This authority is granted narrowly and by name or role, not assumed by virtue of seniority, tenure, or subject-matter expertise. An engineering director with deep knowledge of a product is not automatically authorized to speak to press about it; authorization comes from Corporate Communications, not from expertise alone.

The employees generally authorized to speak on behalf of Contoso Corp are the Chief Executive Officer, the Chief Financial Officer, and other named executive officers for matters within their remit; the Vice President of Corporate Communications and members of that team acting on their behalf; the Vice President of Investor Relations for matters involving financial performance or the investment community; and any employee to whom Corporate Communications has granted written, time-bound approval for a specific engagement, such as a named conference talk or a pre-approved podcast interview.

All other employees, including managers, individual contributors, and even members of the extended leadership team outside this list, are not authorized to issue statements, confirm rumors, comment on financial performance, or characterize company strategy to any external audience, including press, analysts, industry forums, and public social media, without express prior approval from Corporate Communications.

Official company statements, press releases, blog posts, and product announcements are drafted, reviewed, and approved through Corporate Communications in coordination with Legal and, where financial information is involved, Investor Relations and the Office of the CFO. This review exists to catch legal exposure, ensure consistency with prior public statements, and confirm that any forward-looking statements include appropriate disclaimers under the Private Securities Litigation Reform Act.

Employees invited to speak at industry conferences, participate in panels, appear on podcasts, write guest articles, or give interviews in any capacity where they will be identified as a Contoso Corp employee must notify Corporate Communications at least ten business days before the engagement, or as soon as reasonably possible if the invitation arrives with less notice. Corporate Communications will help prepare talking points and, where appropriate, arrange media training.

Slide decks, product demonstrations, and other materials intended for use outside the company at a public event must be reviewed by Corporate Communications and, where product roadmap or unreleased features are involved, by the relevant product and legal teams, before the event. This review is not optional for convenience; it exists because a single leaked roadmap slide has, in the past, forced companies into premature and legally fraught disclosures.

Contoso Corp's Employee Advocacy Program allows employees who want to amplify the company's official voice to do so in a supported way. Employees may opt in to the program through the Corporate Communications intranet portal, which provides a rotating library of pre-approved posts, graphics, and talking points that employees can share or adapt on their own social media accounts.

Content shared through the Employee Advocacy Program should be shared substantially as provided; employees who want to add significant personal commentary beyond a short introductory sentence should treat that commentary as personal opinion subject to the disclaimer requirements described in the Personal Social Media Use section above, rather than as an extension of the official company message.

Employees representing Contoso Corp at trade shows, customer events, or on official company social accounts should never speculate publicly about unannounced products, unconfirmed partnerships, pending litigation, or future financial results, even when directly pressed by an attendee or journalist. The appropriate response is to redirect the question to Corporate Communications, not to offer a personal guess dressed up as company insight.

Managers who supervise employees frequently contacted by external parties, such as those in sales, customer success, or developer relations, are responsible for ensuring their teams understand where the line falls between normal customer conversation, which is expected and encouraged, and public statement-making, which requires authorization. When in doubt, managers should escalate to Corporate Communications rather than making an ad hoc call.

Former employees are reminded that confidentiality, non-disparagement, and trade secret obligations described in the Contoso Corp Code of Conduct and Business Ethics Policy and in individual separation agreements survive termination of employment, and that speaking to media or posting publicly as a 'former Contoso Corp employee' does not relieve a person of those ongoing obligations.

Violations of this section, including unauthorized statements to press, unapproved public commitments about product timelines, or disclosure of confidential deal terms during an external speaking engagement, are treated with the same seriousness as other confidentiality and disclosure violations described in the Contoso Corp Code of Conduct and Business Ethics Policy and may result in outcomes ranging from a documented warning to termination, depending on severity and intent.

Regional offices in Northlake, Austin, and Dublin sometimes receive requests from local press or local industry publications that would not typically rise to the attention of the corporate media relations team in the normal course of business, such as a local business journal profiling the Austin office's expansion. These requests must still be routed through Corporate Communications rather than handled locally, since even a local story can be picked up by wire services or referenced by financial media covering CTSO.

Employees serving on the board of an external nonprofit, industry association, or standards body may reference their Contoso Corp title and employer in that capacity, consistent with normal professional practice, but should not use the external platform to make statements about Contoso Corp's business, products, or strategy without the same Corporate Communications review that would apply to any other public engagement.

Contoso Corp's official corporate social media accounts, including the primary company pages on major platforms, are managed exclusively by the Corporate Communications social media team. Business units, regional offices, and product teams that want a presence on social media must request a company-branded account through Corporate Communications rather than creating one independently, so that the company maintains a consistent visual identity, a single set of login credentials under proper security controls, and one point of accountability for content posted under the Contoso Corp name.

Recruiting-related social media content, including job postings shared by employees and posts describing open roles or team culture, is generally welcome and encouraged as part of informal referral efforts, provided the content is accurate, does not promise compensation or terms that Human Resources has not approved, and does not disparage the hiring process, other candidates, or the team the role sits within.

## Media and Analyst Inquiries

Any employee who is contacted directly by a journalist, blogger, industry analyst, financial analyst, or investor, whether by phone, email, social media direct message, or in person, should not respond to substantive questions themselves. This is true even if the question seems simple, the employee knows the answer, and responding quickly would seem helpful.

The correct response to an unexpected media inquiry is to politely decline to comment on the spot, take down the reporter's name, outlet, and contact information, and forward the request immediately to Corporate Communications at corpcomms@contoso-corp.example. A simple script such as 'I'm not the right person to speak to that, but I'll make sure our communications team follows up with you today' is appropriate and sufficient in almost every case.

This redirect requirement applies regardless of how routine the question appears. A reporter asking what seems like a harmless question about office culture, hiring plans, or a public event can be building toward a story about layoffs, litigation, or financial performance, and an offhand answer from an unauthorized employee can be quoted as if it were an official company position.

Inquiries from financial analysts or institutional investors, including questions about earnings, guidance, capital allocation, or strategic direction, must always be routed to Investor Relations rather than Corporate Communications, since these conversations are governed by Regulation FD and require careful handling to avoid selectively disclosing material nonpublic information to some market participants and not others.

Employees should never confirm or deny a rumor about Contoso Corp, even one that appears to be common knowledge internally or that has already been reported by another outlet, without explicit direction from Corporate Communications or Investor Relations. Confirming a rumor that Corporate Communications has not yet cleared for public discussion can itself be treated as a disclosure event with securities law consequences.

Corporate Communications maintains a rapid-response process for time-sensitive media inquiries and aims to acknowledge every forwarded request within two business hours during standard business days, and as quickly as practicable outside of those hours for urgent matters. Employees who forward a request should include as much detail as they have, including any deadline the reporter mentioned, so Corporate Communications can prioritize appropriately.

For a crisis situation, meaning an event with the potential for significant reputational, legal, financial, safety, or regulatory impact, such as a data breach, a workplace safety incident, a major product outage, or a significant legal action, employees who become aware of media interest or public attention should escalate immediately through the crisis communications hotline listed on the Corporate Communications intranet page, in addition to notifying their manager.

The crisis communications escalation path activates a cross-functional response team that typically includes Corporate Communications, Legal, Investor Relations where financial impact is possible, Information Security where a breach is involved, and the relevant business unit leader, with the Chief Executive Officer or their delegate serving as the ultimate decision-maker on public statements during an active crisis.

During an active crisis, all employees, not just those directly involved in the underlying incident, should refrain from posting about the situation on personal social media, including expressions of concern, speculation about cause, or commentary on the company's response, until Corporate Communications has issued guidance. Well-intentioned personal posts during a crisis have, in other companies' experience, complicated legal strategy and contradicted official statements issued minutes later.

Employees who are approached by media at their home, in public, or through personal channels rather than through work contact information should follow the same redirect process, since a reporter's choice of contact method does not change the sensitivity of the underlying request or the need for a coordinated, accurate company response.

Requests for the company to comment on litigation, whether pending, threatened, or newly filed, must always be routed through Legal in coordination with Corporate Communications, and no employee should offer a personal characterization of the merits of any case involving Contoso Corp, even one they consider frivolous or clearly meritorious, in public or to press.

Corporate Communications periodically offers voluntary media training sessions for employees in customer-facing or public-facing roles who are likely to be approached by press or analysts even though they are not designated spokespeople. Managers of such teams are encouraged to nominate employees for this training so that redirect procedures become second nature rather than a scramble in the moment.

Employees who violate the media and analyst inquiry procedures described here, including by responding substantively to a reporter without authorization, disclosing material nonpublic information to an analyst, or confirming an unannounced rumor, are subject to disciplinary action under the Contoso Corp Code of Conduct and Business Ethics Policy, up to and including termination, and the company reserves the right to pursue additional remedies where the disclosure causes legal or financial harm.

Employees with questions about any part of this policy, or who are uncertain whether a specific planned communication requires review, should contact Corporate Communications before publishing or speaking rather than after, and should consult the Contoso Corp Employee Handbook for general guidance on how this policy fits within the broader framework of workplace conduct expectations.

Analyst day and earnings call preparation is coordinated exclusively by Investor Relations working with the CFO's office, Corporate Communications, and outside securities counsel. Employees outside these groups who are asked to contribute data, slides, or talking points for an analyst day or earnings call should route their contributions through their manager and the assigned Investor Relations liaison, and should not share drafts of earnings materials informally, including internally, beyond the individuals with a defined need to know.

When Contoso Corp receives an inquiry from a regulator, government agency, or law enforcement that also touches on public communications, such as a request connected to a pending investigation that a reporter has also asked about, Legal takes the lead in coordination with Corporate Communications, and no statement of any kind, public or private, should be made about the underlying regulatory matter without Legal's sign-off.

Employee referral of a media inquiry to Corporate Communications is never treated as a failure or an inconvenience; it is the correct outcome the process is designed to produce. Employees will not face any negative consequence for declining to answer a reporter's question and redirecting it, even if the reporter expresses frustration or pressure in the moment, and managers should never suggest to a team member that answering directly would have been the more helpful choice.

During a widely covered industry event, product recall, or competitor crisis where Contoso Corp is asked to comment on a market-wide issue rather than something specific to Contoso Corp, employees should still apply the same redirect procedure, since even a comment on a broader industry trend can be interpreted by media as an implicit statement about Contoso Corp's own position or exposure.

Corporate Communications maintains a standing set of approved boilerplate language describing Contoso Corp, its headquarters in Northlake, Washington, and its history for use in press materials, and employees drafting any external-facing material that includes a company description should pull the current version from the intranet rather than writing a new description from memory, to avoid inadvertent inaccuracies creeping into public materials over time.
